# Why Ranked Robin — the friendly upgrade for ranked ballots

*For anyone who already loves ranked ballots. Ranked Robin keeps the exact ballot you know — rank the candidates in your honest order — and counts it so the candidate a **majority prefers over everyone** wins. Same ballot, a count that finds the consensus. This is the positive case; the [method page](ranked_robin.md) has the mechanics and the [honest limits](RCV_RR_honest_limits.md) are stated plainly at the end.*

→ Companion: [Why STAR Voting](../topics/Why_STAR_Voting.md) (the scored ballot). Both aim past Choose-One; this page is for the ranked-ballot family.

---

## The case in eight points

### 1. Ranked Robin in one sentence

**Whoever would win a one-on-one race against each other candidate wins the election.** Ranked Robin holds every head-to-head matchup at once — a round-robin tournament — and elects the candidate who beats them all: the **consensus winner**.

### 2. You keep the ballot you already believe in

Rank the candidates in your honest order of preference — the same ranked ballot ranked-choice voters already use and love. Nothing to relearn. Ranked Robin is not a different *ballot*; it's a better way to *count* the one you already like.

### 3. The consensus candidate wins

The winner is the one **more voters prefer over each rival, one on one** — the broadly-acceptable choice a majority is happy with, not just the favorite of the largest faction. In a divided race, that's usually the candidate who can actually represent the whole community.

### 4. Every ranking you write counts

Ranked Robin reads your **whole** ballot: every candidate you rank is compared against every other, in every matchup. Your second and third choices are always in play — they never get thrown out, and there's no "my ballot didn't count" surprise. Rank as few or as many as you like.

### 5. Your favorite never hurts you

Ranking your true favorite first is always safe. Ranked Robin is **monotonic** — giving a candidate *more* support can never make them lose — so you're never punished for voting honestly, and you never have to calculate. Vote your heart.

### 6. Easy to count, and verifiable at the precinct

The count is just pairwise tallies, and **they add up precinct by precinct** — each polling place can post its own head-to-head numbers on election night, and the totals combine by simple addition. No shipping every ballot to a central computer; the result is transparent and auditable from local tallies.

### 7. Easy to explain

"The candidate who'd beat each of the others in a one-on-one race wins." Everyone understands a round-robin — it's how sports leagues and chess tournaments crown a champion. No rounds of elimination to follow, no transfers to trace.

### 8. Honest about what it is — and isn't

Ranked Robin captures your **order**, not your **strength** of feeling — it can't tell a squeaker from a landslide. If you want to express *how much* you prefer someone, a scored ballot like [STAR](../topics/Why_STAR_Voting.md) carries more of your voice. And in the rare case of a [Condorcet cycle](cycle_resolution.md) (A beats B beats C beats A, with no one beating everyone), there's no consensus winner and the count falls back on a tiebreak reasonable people can debate. These are real, and small; naming them is part of why the rest is trustworthy.

---

## Where it fits — we're all on the same side

If you love ranked ballots, **Ranked Robin is your upgrade**: the ballot you know, counted so the consensus candidate wins, tallied transparently at the precinct. If you'd also like to say *how strongly* you feel, **[STAR](../topics/Why_STAR_Voting.md)** is the scored cousin. Both are aiming at the same thing — an honest ballot and a winner with real majority support — and both are a world beyond Choose-One.

The point was never to run down anyone's favorite reform. It's that the ranked ballot deserves a count worthy of it, and Ranked Robin is one.

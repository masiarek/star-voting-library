# Wasted Votes — what the phrase actually means (four senses), and how each method fares

"My vote was wasted" is one of the most common complaints in elections — and one of the most *overloaded* phrases in voting theory. It's used for at least four different things, and arguments go in circles when people mean different ones. This page pins the senses down, shows how each method fares, and reads the popular *"STAR has no wasted votes"* claim honestly.

## The four senses of "wasted vote"

1. **Loser + surplus (the plurality sense).** Under [Choose-One / plurality](plurality.md), every vote for a candidate who *loses* had no effect — and so did every vote for a runaway winner *beyond* the number they needed to win. This is the sense behind the gerrymandering **"efficiency gap."** It's the widest kind of waste: in a three-way race, a *majority* of votes can be "wasted" this way.
2. **Exhausted (the RCV-IRV sense).** In [RCV-IRV](RCV_IRV/RCV-IRV-Hare.md), a ballot whose ranked candidates are all eliminated is **[set aside mid-count](RCV_IRV/RCV_IRV_exhausted_ballots.md)** and stops affecting the result — sometimes involuntarily, because a [ranking cap](RCV_IRV/forced_vs_voluntary_exhaustion.md) wouldn't let the voter rank more. A *different* mechanism from #1, often lumped in with it.
3. **The spoiler pressure ("throwing your vote away").** The *fear* that voting for your sincere favorite — a third party, an underdog — will **help elect your worst choice** ([the spoiler effect](spoiler_effect.md)). Here the "waste" is strategic: you feel forced to abandon your favorite for a "lesser evil" who can actually win. This one shapes behavior even when no ballot is literally discarded.
4. **The method claim ("no wasted votes").** Advocacy shorthand — usually meaning a method where you can *support more than one candidate* without the vote-splitting of #1 or #3. This is the sense in slogans, and the one that needs the most care (below).

## How each method fares

| | Loser/surplus waste (#1) | Exhaustion (#2) | Spoiler pressure (#3) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Choose-One / Plurality** | **Worst** — one mark, so most non-winning votes are "wasted" | n/a | **Strongest** — the classic lesser-evil trap |
| **RCV-IRV** | Reduced by transfers | **Yes** — ballots can [exhaust](RCV_IRV/RCV_IRV_exhausted_ballots.md) | Reduced, but [center squeeze](RCV_IRV/RCV_IRV_center_squeeze.md) can still punish a sincere first choice |
| **Approval / STAR / Ranked Robin** | Much less — you can support several candidates at once | **None** — no eliminations, every ballot is read to the end | Much less — supporting a favorite doesn't split your vote |

The honest summary: the **Equal Vote methods** (STAR, Approval, Ranked Robin) sharply cut the *strategic* waste (#3) and don't exhaust ballots (#2), because you're no longer limited to one mark. That's a real, substantial improvement over the status quo. It is **not** the same as "every vote now decides the outcome."

## Reading "STAR has no wasted votes" honestly

It's a strong slogan, and it's mostly defensible — but be precise about *which* sense it means:

**What's true:**
- **No vote-splitting / no spoiler trap (#3).** Scoring every candidate independently means supporting your favorite never helps elect your worst choice. You can vote your conscience.
- **No ballot is discarded (#2).** STAR has no elimination rounds, so nothing "exhausts." A blank counts as **0** and still participates; even a voter with no preference between the two finalists had their scores counted in the Scoring Round.
- **One equal vote in the runoff.** Big scores and small scores count the same in the final, so no ballot is worth less than another there.

**The honest qualifications:**
- **A `0` is a real (lowest) score, not a neutral discard.** Saying "no vote is thrown away" is fairer than in plurality, but a 0 *is* an expressed opinion, not a non-vote. → ["Aren't equal-score votes just discounted?"](STAR_Voting/are_equal_score_votes_discounted.md)
- **[Equal Support](STAR_Voting/are_equal_score_votes_discounted.md) means you chose not to tip the runoff.** If you rate the two finalists the same, your ballot doesn't decide between them — by your own choice, not the method's.
- **You can still be on the losing side.** Your candidate not winning is not the same as your vote being "wasted" — every ballot was counted; the electorate simply preferred someone else. No method makes every voter's choice decisive (that's impossible).

So the precise, defensible claim is: **STAR eliminates *strategic* waste (vote-splitting, the spoiler trap) and never discards a ballot** — not the literal "every vote changes the outcome." Made precisely, it's a strong point; made as an absolute, it overreaches. (Full honest caveats: [STAR's honest limits](STAR_Voting/STAR_honest_limits.md).)

# Who's who in voting methods — a sourced reference

*The names you'll keep running into in social-choice theory and electoral reform, grouped by the tradition or method they're associated with. This is **a reference, not a ranking** — no one here is presented as more correct than anyone else. Where a person advocates a particular method, that's stated plainly as **disclosure** (so you can read their work knowing where they stand), not as endorsement or criticism. The companion page [Do the experts really think RCV-IRV is "bad"?](expert_consensus_and_irv.md) explains why that disclosure matters: the most-cited "measurers" are frequently also advocates — on every side.*

**Level: reference** (a 201/301 companion). See also: [advocacy organizations](advocacy_organizations.md) · [What makes a voting method good?](what_makes_a_voting_method_good.md).

> Facts below are sourced and were current as of mid-2026; roles and affiliations change — verify on the linked pages. Living people are described strictly by verifiable role and contribution.

## Foundational theory (the social-choice tradition)

These are the mathematicians and economists whose results define the whole field. Least contested — mostly historical, and cited by *every* camp.

| Person | Known for | Links |
|---|---|---|
| **Marquis de Condorcet** (1743–1794) | The **Condorcet winner/criterion** (beats every rival head-to-head) and the **Condorcet paradox** (majority preferences can cycle) | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marquis_de_Condorcet) · [electowiki](https://electowiki.org/wiki/Condorcet_criterion) |
| **Jean-Charles de Borda** (1733–1799) | The **Borda count** (points by rank position) | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Charles_de_Borda) |
| **Kenneth Arrow** (1921–2017) | **Arrow's impossibility theorem** — no ranked method satisfies a short list of fairness conditions at once (Nobel, 1972; Stanford) | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Arrow) |
| **Duncan Black** (1908–1991) | The **median voter theorem**; a founder of public-choice theory | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duncan_Black) |
| **Allan Gibbard** & **Mark Satterthwaite** | The **Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem** — every reasonable non-dictatorial method is manipulable (no method is fully strategy-proof) | [Gibbard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allan_Gibbard) · [Satterthwaite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Satterthwaite) |
| **Peter Fishburn** (1936–2021) | Decision theory; co-developed the modern case for **Approval voting** with Brams | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_C._Fishburn) |
| **Michel Balinski** (1933–2019) | Apportionment theory; **Majority Judgment** (with Rida Laraki) | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Balinski) |
| **Nicolaus Tideman** | **Ranked Pairs** (a Condorcet method); Virginia Tech economist | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaus_Tideman) |
| **Markus Schulze** | The **Schulze method** (a widely-used Condorcet method) | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schulze_method) |

## How to use this page

- **Note the affiliation before you weigh the claim.** A result from an advocate isn't wrong *because* they advocate — but "an expert says method M is best" lands differently when the expert founded the M campaign. This cuts every direction, STAR included.
- **Prefer the primary link.** The bios and org pages above are more current than any summary here.
- **The theory names are the neutral ground.** Arrow, Gibbard–Satterthwaite, and Condorcet are cited by all camps precisely because they're impossibility/analysis results, not advocacy.

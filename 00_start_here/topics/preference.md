# "Preference" — the Word That Causes Half the Confusion

*"Preferential Voting" (Australia's name for RCV-IRV) is a misnomer. Here's why, and what "preference" actually means.*

---

The trouble is that **"preference" has two different meanings**, and voting debates slide between them constantly:

1. **Everyday meaning — your subjective opinion.** "I prefer A" = "I like A." This is rich and includes *how much* you like each option, your indifferences, your hesitations.
2. **Technical (social-choice) meaning — a ranking.** In the academic literature a "preference" is a **complete, transitive ordering** of the candidates — i.e., literally a *ranking*. Following Arrow, "preference" and "ranking" are often used interchangeably. (The social-choice scholar Eric Pacuit notes he deliberately says *"ranking"* rather than *"preference"* to avoid exactly this confusion.)

The key insight: a **ballot only captures *some aspect* of your preference**, and you shouldn't identify the two. Your subjective opinion is the full thing; a ranked ballot records only the *order*, a scored ballot records *order + strength*, a choose-one ballot records only your *favorite*. None of them *is* your preference — each is a lossy snapshot of it.

## The four ways a ballot can ask for your "preference"

- **Choose one** candidate (plurality).
- **Choose several** (approval — a limited number, or as many as you like).
- **Rank** them (1st, 2nd, 3rd…).
- **Rate / score** them (A is 5, B is 2…).

All four express a "preference" in the everyday sense. So naming *one* of them "Preferential Voting" claims a general word for a single specific method.

## Why "Preferential Voting" is a misnomer

Australia calls **RCV-IRV** "Preferential Voting" — and it's misleading on two levels:

- **It implies it's *the* way to express preferences.** But every method above expresses preferences. Approval, Score, and STAR voters are expressing preferences too. The name quietly suggests the others don't.
- **"Preference" is broader than "ranking."** In the everyday sense, a preference includes **strength** ("I love A, tolerate B"), which a **score** captures but a **rank** throws away. So "Preferential Voting" attaches the *broad* concept (which covers scoring) to the *narrow* mechanism (strict ranking) — the exact opposite of clarifying. (See [scores_vs_ranks.md](../scores_and_ranks/scores_vs_ranks.md).)

The same word trips people elsewhere too: a **"preference ballot"** is ambiguous (ranks? scores?), and "preferences" can even mislead about *intent* — a judge or a voter may be expressing what they believe is **right**, not merely what they personally fancy.

## The honest takeaway

"Preferential Voting," like "Ranked Choice Voting," is a broad-sounding label glued to one specific method (RCV-IRV / Hare). When precision matters, name the **ballot** (ranked vs. scored) and the **count** (IRV elimination vs. Condorcet pairwise vs. score sum) — not "preference," which is too slippery to identify a method on its own.

# RCV-IRV in North Carolina — a short-lived experiment (2006–2013)

*North Carolina ran one of the earliest and boldest US trials of instant-runoff
voting — including the **first statewide general election ever decided by IRV** — then
repealed it. A useful, real case of IRV adopted, tested at scale, and reversed.*

→ Broader context: [origins & spread](RCV_IRV_history.md) · the method:
[RCV-IRV (Hare)](RCV-IRV-Hare.md) · another real case: [Alaska 2022](RCV_IRV_alaska_2022.md)

## Timeline

- **2006 — enacted.** The General Assembly authorized IRV for certain judicial
  vacancies and set up a **pilot program** — up to 10 cities in 2007 and up to 10
  counties in 2008 — to try ranked ballots in real elections.
- **2007 — first pilots (Cary, Hendersonville).** **Cary** used IRV in a municipal
  race; because the voting machines couldn't tabulate ranked ballots, the runoff rounds
  were **hand-counted**, which delayed results. **Hendersonville** also piloted it and
  reported a positive administrative and voter experience, and kept using it for several
  later elections.
- **2010 — the big one: a statewide Court of Appeals seat.** A **13-candidate**,
  nonpartisan, **statewide** race to fill the vacancy left by James Wynn — believed to be
  the **first time IRV/RCV decided any statewide general election in the United States.**
- **2013 — repealed.** The pilot program was ended by the General Assembly. The repeal
  was tucked into a large omnibus bill (**H589**, the so-called "monster bill," best known
  for introducing voter-ID requirements). Special judicial elections with more than two
  candidates reverted to plain plurality.

## The 2010 statewide election — the first-round leader lost

This is the race everyone remembers, because the outcome flipped between rounds:

- **First round:** **Cressie Thigpen** — the sitting judge, favored by Democrats — led
  with about **395,000** of the ~2 million votes cast, roughly **100,000 ahead** of
  second-place **Doug McCullough** (favored by Republicans). But with 13 candidates,
  Thigpen was **far short of a majority**.
- **After the IRV rounds:** as low candidates were eliminated and their voters' next
  choices were distributed, **McCullough overtook Thigpen** and won the third round
  **543,980 to 537,325** — a margin of about **0.62%**.

So the candidate who **led the first count lost** after the transfers. To IRV's
supporters that's the method working exactly as intended — a plurality leader without a
majority shouldn't be guaranteed the win. To critics it looked like the system
**overturning the front-runner** in a way voters found confusing, and it became a
rallying point against the pilot. Either reading, it's a clean real-world example that
**the first-choice leader is not always the IRV winner** — the same lesson as
[Alaska 2022](RCV_IRV_alaska_2022.md), here without a clear center-squeeze story.

## Why it was repealed — the debate

The critics' case (the framing in NC anti-IRV advocacy) was mostly **administrative and
about legitimacy**, not the social-choice pathologies:

- **Machines couldn't count it** — ranked ballots had to be hand-tabulated centrally,
  adding cost, labor, and delay, and making **audits and recounts** more involved (IRV is
  [not precinct-summable](RCV_IRV_lack_of_summability.md), so a recount re-runs the rounds).
- **Voter confusion** and the surprise of a first-round leader losing.
- The pilot was small and intermittent, so it never built durable public familiarity.

IRV's proponents (e.g. FairVote) countered that the pilots ran smoothly where machines
allowed, saved the cost of a separate runoff election, and produced majority-tested
winners. The repeal itself was uncontroversial to pass because it rode along in a much
larger, higher-profile bill.

The lasting significance: NC remains the **only US state to have decided a statewide
general election by IRV**, and its arc — adopt, pilot, scale up, repeal — is a
frequently-cited data point in US ranked-voting debates.

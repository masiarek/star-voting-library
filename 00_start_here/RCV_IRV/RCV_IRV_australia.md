# The Alternative Vote in Australia — what a century actually proves

Australia is the world's longest-running home of single-winner IRV, so both its advocates and its critics reach for it as evidence. This page tries to be the impartial referee: Australia settles one big question decisively, and a *different* big question in the opposite direction from what US advocates often imply. Keeping those two straight is the whole point.

*(Terminology: Australians call it the **Alternative Vote (AV)** or **preferential voting** — their names for single-winner IRV. This page uses those, not the US term "RCV." See [the naming problem](RCV-IRV-confusing-name.md) and [variants](RCV_IRV_variants.md).)*

## What Australia actually uses

- **House of Representatives:** the **Alternative Vote** (single-winner IRV) since **1918** — over a century of continuous national use.
- **Senate:** **STV** (the *proportional*, multi-winner cousin) since 1948 — a different method, and the source of most of Australia's multiparty representation.
- **Compulsory voting** since 1924, and (historically) **full preferential numbering** required — both of which shape the outcomes and make naïve cross-country comparisons misleading (below).

## What Australia settles: IRV is durable and workable — *yes*

On the question *"can single-winner IRV run a national democracy, stably, for the long haul?"* Australia is about as strong a "yes" as empirical evidence gets:

- **100+ years** of uninterrupted use, administered at national scale without a legitimacy crisis over the method itself.
- Voters and officials handle preferential ballots routinely; hand-counting and reporting are well-established.
- Minor-party and independent candidates can **run without being pure spoilers**, because second preferences flow.

Anyone claiming IRV is unworkable or too complex for real elections has to explain Australia. It's fair, and important, to say so plainly.

## What Australia does *not* settle: it did **not** break two-party dominance

This is where the common US framing overreaches. A century of AV did **not** turn Australia's lower house into a multiparty chamber:

- The House has remained a **two-party-preferred (2PP)** contest between **Labor** and the **Coalition** — so entrenched that "**two-party-preferred vote**" is the standard metric the Australian Electoral Commission itself reports.
- The **Greens** draw a large first-preference share (well into double digits) but win only a **handful of lower-house seats** — a persistent gap between votes and seats that IRV does nothing to close.
- The genuinely multiparty part of Australian politics lives in the **Senate — which uses STV (proportional), not IRV.** Attributing Australia's minor-party representation to the Alternative Vote gets the mechanism wrong; it's the *proportional* method doing that work.

The honest reason is structural: AV is still a **single-member-district, majoritarian** system, so — like all such systems ([Duverger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duverger%27s_law)) — it tends toward two dominant blocs. It removes the *spoiler* problem within that structure; it does not make the structure proportional.

**A fair nuance the other way:** AV doesn't *mechanically enforce* two-party outcomes either. Australia's **2022** federal election returned a record crossbench of independents ("teals") and Greens, showing the lower house *can* diversify under preferential voting when the electorate shifts. So the accurate statement is "tends strongly two-party, doesn't lock it in" — not "two parties forever."

## Caveats that make cross-country claims tricky

- **Turnout isn't an IRV effect.** Australia's famously high turnout comes from **compulsory voting**, not the ballot type — don't credit it to AV.
- **Exhaustion is low there for a reason.** Historically Australia required ranking *all* candidates (full preferential), so ballots rarely [exhaust](RCV_IRV_exhausted_ballots.md) — unlike US "rank up to 5" systems where [forced exhaustion](forced_vs_voluntary_exhaustion.md) is common. Optional-preferential states (e.g. NSW) show more exhaustion. The US IRV experience and the Australian one differ partly *because the ballot rules differ*.
- **Ballot-order effects** ("donkey votes") and the 2PP reporting convention itself are Australian-specific artifacts a US comparison should flag.

## The even-handed takeaway

> Australia proves single-winner IRV is **durable and workable** at national scale. It is **not** evidence that IRV **breaks two-party dominance** or yields **multiparty democracy** — Australia's multiparty representation comes from its **Senate's STV**, a proportional method. Cite Australia for the first claim; stop citing it for the second.

Both camps overuse Australia. The workability is real and IRV's defenders are right to point to it; the "it'll break the two-party system" hope is exactly what a century of Australian data does *not* support.
